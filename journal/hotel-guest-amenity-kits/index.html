Choosing a Hotel Guest Amenity Kit

What 4- and 5-star properties actually look for in a leather amenity kit. The brief, the budget, the timeline, and the questions to ask before signing.

Most hotel amenity kit conversations start with a budget number and a hand-wave. They should start with the guest. Whether a kit succeeds in a property is a function of three things — what the guest takes home, what the property looks like through it, and whether the procurement team can re-order it next year at the same per-unit cost. Everything else is detail.

Start with the guest, not the budget

The first question on any amenity kit brief is not "how much do we want to spend?" It is "where in the stay does the guest open this?" The answer determines almost everything else.

  • Pre-arrival or welcome amenity — opened at check-in or first turndown. The leather is the first physical brand touch the guest has. Card holders, luggage tags, and passport sleeves work here. They are also small enough that the cost-per-room stays manageable.
  • Concierge gifting — handed to a VIP arrival, repeat guest, or recovery scenario. A larger kit with a hand-tattooed personalisation can carry the moment.
  • Loyalty milestone — shipped to the guest's home address after a stay. Removes the suitcase-volume problem and lets the property send something larger.
  • Group, board, or wedding — a single occasion, a fixed headcount, a higher per-unit budget. The most flexible category and the most overlooked.

The budget reality

For a serious leather amenity kit — full grain leather, hot-stamped property crest, presentation packaging — the realistic cost-per-piece bands are:

  • Card holder, single piece: $35–$60 per unit at volume
  • Luggage tag: $25–$45 per unit at volume
  • Passport sleeve: $50–$80 per unit at volume
  • Three-piece welcome kit (above three pieces in a linen envelope): $110–$185 per unit at volume
  • Holster bag, per piece: $130–$220 per unit at volume
  • Bespoke flagship kit with custom hardware: $300+

If a supplier quotes you significantly below these bands, the leather is split, bonded, or PU-coated. There is nothing wrong with that for a 3-star property — there is everything wrong with it for a property charging $1,500 a night.

The kit you give a guest is the version of your brand they take home. It outlasts the room, the meal, and the staff turnover.

The timeline reality

Six to ten weeks from approved brief to delivery is the honest range. A two-week sample window, a one-week approval window, six to eight weeks of production and air freight. Programmes promising delivery in four weeks are either holding inventory of generic pieces (and stamping a logo on top) or skipping leather curing time.

Property openings are the exception. A rush programme can be quoted at four weeks, but it costs 30 to 50 percent more, and the leather is selected for what is already in the tannery rather than dyed to your spec.

Questions to ask the supplier

  1. Do you make this leather, or do you finish it? Many "leather goods suppliers" are converters — they buy finished leather and cut it. Ask whose tannery the leather comes from. The answer should be specific.
  2. What's the failure rate on hot-stamping at this volume? A real number is between 2 and 5 percent. If the supplier says zero, they are guessing. If they say more than 8, the press is wrong for the leather.
  3. What does the lifetime of the kit look like? Will the leather crack at year three? Will the foil rub off the crest at year one? Ask for samples that are at least a year old. A serious supplier keeps these.
  4. Re-order at the same per-unit? Lock the bill of materials in writing. The supplier should hold per-unit pricing for at least 24 months on a re-order — otherwise next year's kit is a new project.
  5. What's the minimum? Most luxury programmes start at 100 to 200 pieces. Anyone offering 25-piece minimums is probably stamping pre-made stock.
  6. Vendor onboarding? The supplier should already be on Avendra, Hilton Supply Management, Marriott SupplierOne, or your hotel group's procurement system. If you have to onboard them from scratch, build six to eight weeks for that.

The packaging matters more than the leather, sometimes

An honest observation from many guest-kit conversations: the unboxing matters as much as the contents. A serious leather card holder loses half its impact in a generic cardboard box. A modest passport sleeve gains half its impact wrapped in tissue, sealed with a wax seal, and presented on the room's writing desk under a handwritten card.

For most properties, allocating ten percent of the kit budget to the presentation — paper, ribbon, wax, card stock, the hand that ties it — is the highest-leverage line item.
