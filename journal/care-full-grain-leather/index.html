How to Care for Full Grain Leather

A simple routine that keeps full grain calfskin looking its best for a lifetime — and the three things that ruin it fastest.

Most leather care advice is overcomplicated. The basics fit on the back of a postcard. What follows is the routine we recommend to clients who buy a Goldy Brown piece — and the routine the South Indian master artisans we work with use on their own bench tools.

The four-step routine

1. Wipe after use

A clean, dry, soft cloth after every use. Cotton t-shirt material works perfectly. The aim is to remove the day's dust and the natural oils your skin has transferred — both of which become abrasive over time if left to settle into the grain.

2. Condition twice a year

A small amount — pea-sized, not more — of neutral leather balm, worked into the leather in small circular motions with a soft cloth. Let it sit for 10 minutes. Buff dry. Twice a year is enough for most clients; quarterly if you live somewhere very dry (Dubai, Phoenix, Calgary in winter) or very humid (Miami, Singapore).

We recommend a neutral balm rather than a coloured one. Coloured conditioners are designed to mask wear; neutral balm preserves it.

3. Avoid sun and salt

Direct sunlight for hours a day, year-round, will dry out and crack any leather. Saltwater — ocean spray, road salt, sweat — will do the same faster. Neither is fatal in occasional contact; neither should be a daily condition. If you live coastal, condition more often.

4. Embrace the patina

Full grain calfskin is supposed to age. The surface darkens, the grain deepens, the colour develops. The piece you carry every day in year three will look richer than the piece you took out of the box in year zero. The patina is not damage. It is the point.

If your leather looks worse with use, it isn't full grain. If it looks better, you've got the real thing.

What ruins leather fastest

  1. Heat. A car dashboard in summer. A radiator. A hairdryer used to "speed up" drying after rain. Heat above about 60°C (140°F) breaks the bonds in the tanning agent. Once that happens, the leather dries permanently and cracks across the grain.
  2. Saturation. Wet leather isn't the problem. Soaked leather is. If a piece gets caught in heavy rain, blot the surface with a dry cloth, stuff the interior with paper to hold its shape, and let it air-dry away from heat. Don't condition while wet — wait until fully dry, then condition.
  3. Bad conditioner. Mink oil and saddle soap are designed for working tack — boots, saddles, working harness. They are too greasy for fine leather goods and they will darken full grain calfskin permanently. Use a balm rated for fine leather.
