What Counts as a Personal Item on a Flight

Carry-on rules across the major carriers in 2026 — and what gate agents actually flag.

Most travellers learn the carry-on rules by being scolded at a gate. The published policies, the actual enforcement, and the unwritten conventions are three different things. What follows is a working guide based on the published 2026 policies of major North American, European, and Gulf carriers, cross-checked against the on-the-ground experience of frequent flyers.

This is not legal advice. Airline policy is the airline's, and a gate agent has the final word at the gate. But the patterns hold.

The two-bag standard

Almost every full-service carrier uses a two-bag rule for cabin baggage: one carry-on (the rolling case in the overhead bin) plus one personal item (the bag under the seat in front of you). Both have published size limits.

Carry-on rules above are published. Verify before flying — they shift quietly. Low-cost carriers (Ryanair, easyJet, Spirit, Frontier) are far stricter and most charge for anything beyond a personal item.

The worn-accessory exception

None of the published policies count a worn item against your carry-on or personal-item allowance. A jacket, a hat, a scarf, a fanny pack, a money belt, a sling, a body-worn travel rig — all of these are treated by every major carrier as something you are wearing rather than something you are carrying.

This is the unwritten rule that most articles miss. The defining test isn't shape, weight, or volume. It is whether the item leaves your body when you sit down. A messenger bag worn over the shoulder, taken off at the seat, and stowed under the seat in front of you is a personal item. A holster worn over the shoulder, stays on through the flight, and never leaves your body is a worn accessory.

The carry-on count is for things that go in a bin or under a seat. Things that stay on your body don't count.

What gate agents actually flag

In several hundred flights of personal travel and conversations with cabin crew and gate agents, the items that get pulled out of the line for being "third bags" are almost always one of three things:

  • A backpack worn on the front of the body in addition to a roller and a personal item
  • A duty-free bag larger than a small carry size — these get flagged at low-cost carriers in Europe especially
  • An obvious tote or shopping bag carried alongside the rolling case and the personal item

Items rarely flagged: cameras around the neck, money belts, fanny packs (worn), DSLR holsters, slings worn under jackets, leather chest rigs worn under jackets. These read as worn accessories.

The Gulf carriers and the long-haul

Emirates, Etihad, Qatar, and Saudia have a quirk worth knowing: their first and business class allowances are substantially more generous than economy, and the personal item limit is enforced more loosely in the front cabins. In economy, the rules are often stricter than the equivalent US carriers. The same chest rig that goes unremarked on a Delta domestic may get a second look from a Doha gate agent on an Etihad economy flight to Sydney.

The fix is to wear it. A worn item that sits flush against the body under a jacket goes through every gate without remark.
