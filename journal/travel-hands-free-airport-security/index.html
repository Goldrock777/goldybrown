How to Travel Hands-Free Through Airport Security

A practical guide to body-worn travel bags, the unwritten rules of carry-on, and the difference between a third bag and a worn accessory.

Most travel guides will tell you to pack a backpack as your personal item. Most travel guides have not stood in a security line at JFK Terminal 4 at six in the morning trying to fish a passport out of a backpack while a TSA officer waits.

The body-worn travel bag is the older, quieter answer. It is what flight crews have used for decades — fanny packs and money belts and chest rigs — and what an increasing number of frequent flyers now wear under a jacket. It holds the things you need at a checkpoint and the things you reach for in the air. Nothing else.

What you actually need at security

Strip the inventory back. The items a security officer ever looks at are: passport or ID, boarding pass, phone. Everything else is laptop bag and overhead bin. The body-worn bag is for those three things, plus a few personal items you don't want to surrender to the bin: AirPods, sunglasses, a slim wallet, a card holder.

If your bag has to fit a laptop, it is no longer a body-worn bag. It is a laptop bag. Carry both. The body-worn bag goes through the metal detector with you; the laptop bag goes on the belt.

The third-bag question

The single question every traveller asks: does a body-worn bag count as a third bag? The honest answer is that it depends on the gate agent, but in practical experience, almost never.

The unwritten rule is that an item worn on the body — a fanny pack, money belt, sling, or shoulder rig — is treated as a worn accessory, not luggage. Airline carry-on rules are written for things you carry. A jacket with deep pockets does not count as a third bag. Neither does a leather chest rig under that jacket.

The test isn't shape or volume. The test is whether the item leaves your body when you sit down.

Bags that come off and sit on a tray fall into the bag count. Bags that stay on you — under a sport coat, over a shell — almost never do. We've worn ours through over two hundred flights without it being counted; the only time it was flagged, the gate agent looked at it and waved us on.

Hardware and metal detectors

The other repeated question is whether the metal hardware sets off detectors. It doesn't. Stainless steel buckles and zippers are the same kind found in millions of belts, watches, and handbags that pass through security every day. Detectors are calibrated to that ambient mass of stainless. They are not calibrated to a few stainless rivets in a leather strap.

The hardware to avoid in a travel bag is brass and large iron-content buckles. They are heavier and they trip detectors more often. Stainless is the standard for a reason.

What to wear under what

The body-worn bag works in three configurations:

  1. Over a base layer, under a jacket. The cleanest line. The bag disappears entirely. This is the configuration we recommend for business travel and red-eyes — it lets you sleep without a strap pressing into your collarbone.
  2. Over a shirt, no jacket. The summer configuration. Visible. Reads as deliberate rather than tactical because the leather is full grain and the hardware is restrained.
  3. Over a jacket. Avoid. It looks busy and the strap rides over the shoulder seam awkwardly. Save for ski lifts.
