Glossary

What Is Vegetable Tanning?

Tannins from tree bark, weeks in stone vats, sun and air. The oldest tanning method still in commercial use — and the one behind most structured luxury leather.

The short answer

Vegetable tanning is the process of converting raw hide into leather using tannins extracted from tree bark and plant material. The hide is soaked in vats of progressively stronger tannin solution for between three and eight weeks, depending on the thickness of the leather and the house tradition. The result is a dense, structured leather that holds its shape, takes tooling, and develops a patina with use.

The agents

Different tanning regions use different bark sources:

  • Wattle and mimosa — fast-growing trees from Africa and Australia, the most common modern tannin source
  • Chestnut and oak — traditional European tannins, used in Tuscan and German tanning
  • Quebracho — South American hardwood, gives a deep red leather
  • Myrobalan and avaram — Indian tanning trees, used in the Tamil Nadu and Kerala tannery belt
  • Hemlock and sumac — historical North American sources, mostly out of use commercially

Many tanneries blend two or three sources to balance speed, colour, and final hand-feel. The blend is part of the house's signature.

The process

  1. Liming and unhairing. Raw hides are soaked in lime to swell the fibres and loosen hair, which is then scraped off. The hide is split to working thickness.
  2. Tanning vats. The hides move through a series of pits or drums of progressively stronger tannin solution. Three to eight weeks total. Each pit deepens the chemical bond between the tannin and the hide protein.
  3. Sun-drying. After the vats, the leather is washed, partially dried in the sun, and oiled.
  4. Currying and conditioning. The leather is rolled, beaten, and worked with oils and waxes to bring it to its final hand. This stage is where artisanal handwork separates one tannery's leather from another's.

Why it costs more

Time, principally. Vegetable tanning takes weeks where chrome tanning takes hours. The tannery has to hold inventory, keep the vats running, and pay for the labour involved in moving hides through them. Tannin extraction itself is also more expensive than chrome salts on a per-unit-of-leather basis.

The result is that veg-tan leather typically costs two to four times more per square foot than chrome-tanned leather of the same weight class.

What it gives you

  • Structure. Veg-tan holds shape. It is the right leather for belts, holsters, briefcases, sheaths, and saddle work.
  • Tooling. Hot-stamping, hand-tooling, embossing, and edge burnishing all work better on veg-tan than on chrome.
  • Patina. Veg-tan leather darkens dramatically with use. A tan briefcase becomes a deep cognac in two years; a cognac one becomes oxblood in five.
  • Smell. Veg-tan smells of leather. Chrome-tanned leather is closer to a chemical neutral.

What it doesn't do well

Veg-tan is not the right leather for every piece. It is too stiff for a soft handbag, too heavy for garment leather, and too prone to water-staining for a piece worn in heavy weather. Chrome tanning solves all three of those problems. Most full collections of luxury leather goods use both methods, picking per piece.

Vegetable tanning at Goldy Brown

The full grain calfskin we use for the structured pieces — the holster body, briefcase walls, document folio covers — is vegetable-tanned in artisan tannery houses in Tamil Nadu using a blend of wattle, myrobalan, and avaram. The tanning takes between four and six weeks. The result is leather that holds the shape of the piece and deepens visibly through the first year of use.
