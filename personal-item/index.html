A Personal Item Bag You Wear, Not Carry

The Goldy Brown Holster is a body-worn leather travel bag that holds your passport, phone, AirPods, and slim wallet at the chest under a jacket. Worn accessories typically do not count against the carry-on or personal item allowance — meaning you can bring it alongside both a roller and a backpack without a third-bag conversation.

The defining test The carry-on count is for things that go in a bin or under a seat. Things that stay on your body — money belts, fanny packs, slings, leather chest rigs — are worn accessories. The Holster is the latter, by design.

What a "personal item" actually is

Almost every full-service airline allows two cabin items: one carry-on (the rolling case in the overhead bin) and one personal item (the bag under the seat in front of you). The personal item is for the things you reach for during the flight — passport, phone, headphones, snacks, a book.

Most travellers fill the personal item slot with a backpack or tote. Both work, but both have the same problem: they leave your body when you sit down. Your passport, your phone, your wallet — all of those go under the seat ahead of you, three rows from your face when you wake up at JFK.

A body-worn personal item solves this. It stays at your chest the whole flight. The four things you need at the gate, on the plane, and at customs are exactly where you left them.

The Holster pouch is 7" × 5" (18 × 13 cm) — well inside every personal item allowance above. Worn on the body, it's a worn accessory. Stowed in another bag, it counts as part of that bag, not a separate item.

The right personal item isn't the largest one you can squeeze under the seat. It's the one that holds the things you actually use on the flight.

What fits in the Holster pouch

  • Passport (every nationality and chip-passport thickness)
  • iPhone 17 Pro Max with case (and every smaller phone)
  • AirPods Pro
  • Slim wallet or card holder (up to 8 cards plus folded cash)
  • Sunglasses (slim frames; sport frames may not)
  • Boarding pass (paper or printed copy)
  • Pen or stylus
  • Folded headphones (over-ear: no — for those, carry separately)

What it doesn't hold: a laptop, a book, a magazine, a water bottle, snacks. For those, you still want a backpack, a tote, or a wheeled carry-on. The Holster is the third piece worn on top — the one that holds the things you don't want to lose track of.

Why a "travelling compartment" (worn) beats a backpack (carried)

The travelling compartment — the dedicated section of any travel bag that holds documents and small electronics — is the part of any travel bag that gets used most. Better travel bags think about that compartment first; lesser ones treat it as an afterthought, and you end up rummaging at the gate.

The Holster is, in effect, just the travelling compartment, lifted out of a larger bag and made wearable. Three advantages:

  1. Always reachable. The compartment is on you, not under a seat or in an overhead. One zip and you're at your passport.
  2. Doesn't count against your bag allowance. Worn accessories don't count. You keep your roller and your backpack.
  3. Sleeps with you. On a redeye, the compartment travels at your collarbone instead of three rows back.
