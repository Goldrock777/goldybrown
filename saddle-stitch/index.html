What Is a Saddle Stitch?

Two needles, one waxed thread, every hole pulled by hand from both sides. The strongest stitch in leatherwork.

The short answer

The saddle stitch is a hand-stitching technique used in fine leatherwork. The maker pre-pierces each hole with an awl, then sews the seam with two needles working from opposite sides — one thread, both ends, every hole pulled simultaneously. The result is a stitch that runs evenly on both sides of the leather and does not unravel if the thread breaks at one point.

How it differs from machine stitching

A sewing machine uses two threads — a top thread and a bobbin thread — locked together inside the leather by a small loop on every stitch. This is called a lockstitch. It is fast (a leather machine can sew thousands of stitches a minute) and consistent. It also has one weakness: if a single stitch breaks, the lock holding the next stitch can slip, and the seam can unravel for an inch or more before catching.

The saddle stitch uses a single thread looped through every hole twice — once from each side. If the thread breaks at one stitch, the surrounding stitches stay locked because each one is independently tied. This is the structural reason the saddle stitch is the standard for fine leather goods that have to last a lifetime.

How a saddle stitch is made

  1. Mark the line. A wing divider scores a guide line about 3mm in from the edge.
  2. Pierce. A pricking iron — a tool with two to ten chisel-shaped teeth — is hammered along the line to make evenly spaced slits in the leather.
  3. Thread. A single length of waxed linen or polyester thread, about four times the seam length, is threaded with a needle on each end.
  4. Stitch. The needle from the front goes through the first hole, the needle from the back goes through the same hole on the opposite side. Both threads are pulled tight. Repeat through the next hole. Both needles cross every hole.
  5. Backstitch and burn. At the end of the seam, the thread is reversed for two or three stitches and the tail is melted into the previous stitch with a small flame.
The saddle stitch is slow because it has to be. A machine cannot replicate the cross-pull tension that makes the seam permanent.

Why luxury houses still saddle-stitch

It is slower (a skilled maker stitches around 60 to 100 stitches an hour by hand, against thousands per minute on a machine), it is harder (the apprenticeship to stitch a clean line takes years), and it costs more (the labour is often the largest line item on a hand-stitched bag). The reason houses still do it is that nothing else gives the same result.

A saddle-stitched seam wears in instead of out. The thread settles into the leather over the first month of use; the stitch line becomes part of the piece rather than sitting on top of it. A machine seam, even a beautifully executed one, sits proud of the leather and will eventually wear unevenly.

The slant of the stitch

Look closely at a hand-saddle-stitched seam and you will see that each stitch sits at a slight angle — usually about 30 degrees off the seam line. This is not random. The pricking iron is angled so the holes are diamond-shaped, and the cross-pull of two needles seats the thread along the long axis of each hole. The slant is the visible signature of a hand-stitched seam.

Machine lockstitches sit perfectly straight along the seam line. If you see a "saddle stitch" with no slant, it is machine-made.
